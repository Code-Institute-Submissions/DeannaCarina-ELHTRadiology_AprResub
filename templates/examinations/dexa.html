Why do you need this examination?

Dexa scans are a quick and easy way to determine a persons' bone density. Your referring physician will want you to have this examination to either confirm or rule out the possibility of having bones that are less dense than they should be normally. A lack of bone density can be caused by a number of reasons, including (but not limited to) medication, age, diet and injury. Once your referring physician knows if you have a lack of bone density, they can then ensure you are given treatment to correct or reduce the symptoms/side effects of this pathology.

Your referring physician should have discussed with you why this examination is necessary for the management and progression of your care and diagnosis, if you are unsure why you need this examination, please discuss this further with your referring physician.

Is it safe?

Dexa scans use radiation to create an image - much like x-rays, however the dose you will receive from this type of examination is extremely low and is roughly the equivalent of 3 hours worth of natural background radiation (about the same as you would receive for a hand x-ray). In most cases, two areas will be images during a dexa scan to determine the density of your bones: your hip and your lower spine, however if you have had surgery on either of these areas and have any metalwork in that area, the computer will interpret this as being extra-dense and not give an accurate and diagnostic result, so it is extremely important to advise your dexa practitioner on any previous surgery you may have had, otherwise you may need to receive more radiation than is necessary.

There is always a risk with any diagnostic (or therapeutic) intervention that uses radiation. Your referring physician has been trained to weigh up the risks and benefits of the dose of radiation you will receive in the examination that has been requested for you.

What do you need to do during the examination?

When you attend for your examination in the dexa department, your practitioner will tell you exactly what you need to do. Depending on the examination you need and whether you are having your hip, spine or wrist imaged will change how we carry out your examination. For lumbar spine images, you will be asked to lay on your back with your lower legs lifted on a block, for a hip image, you will be layed flat with your legs straight, and for a wrist image you will usually be sat on a chair at the side of the table (but not always).

It is very important that when the practitioner is happy with your positioning, you stay extremely still until they tell you to move again. Moving before or during your imaging may result in an un-diagnostic image which would mean it needs to be taken again, resulting in you receiving more radiation.

After your scan, the practitioner will confirm you know where and how to get your results. If you were changed into a gown for your examination, you will now be able to get changed back into your normal clothes and carry on with your day as normal.

What will the practitioner do during the examination?

Your dexa practitioner will firstly confirm that you are the correct person for this examination, they will ask you to confirm your details and to confirm you understand what you are in the department for - these are standard questions that we must ask everybody.

They will then take your height and weight as these are added to the calculations when measuring your bone density, and check your medical history to see if you have had any surgery on your spine, hip or wrist. They will then position you for your dexa scan, they will tell you whether you need to sit or lay down for your imaging, and they may need to touch you to ensure you are in the correct place. Once they are happy with your position, you will be asked to stay very still while they take your image, depending on the layout of the dexa room, they may stay with you while your image is being taken - this usually takes about 5 minutes. Once your scan has been taken, they will check they have everything they need on your pictures (this includes ensuring all your personal details have been transferred over correctly, that you haven't moved during imaging, that all of the area of interest is on the image and that the reporters will be able to make a firm diagnosis from your scan) this can take some time, and you should try and stay in the same position while the practitioner is doing this just in case they need another image to aid in a better diagnosis.
