Why do you need this examination?

CT scans are a quick and easy way to diagnose a range of pathologies and are usually minimally invasive for patients. Your referring physician will want you to have this examination to either confirm or rule out a possible diagnosis. Where plain film x-rays produce an image on a single plane, CT scans are able to produce images on multiple planes to be able to provide a more in depth view of the area of interest. The most common reasons to have a CT scan are: to diagnose muscle and bone disorders, such as bone tumors and fractures, to pinpoint the location of a tumor, infection or blood clot or to guide procedures such as surgeries and biopsies.

Your referring physician should have discussed with you why this examination is necessary for the management and progression of your care and diagnosis, if you are unsure why you need this examination, please discuss this further with your referring physician.

Is it safe?

The dose of radiation you will receive during an CT examination will entirely depend on the area of the body that is being imaged. The dose you would receive from a CT scan are much more than those received from a plain film x-ray and as such, the radiation related risks from this examination are higher. To put things in perspective, the average dose for a plain film chest x-ray is equivalent to roughly 10 days of natural background radiation, the radiation dose you would receive from a CT scan of your chest is roughly the equivalent of 2 years worth of natural background radiation.

There is always a risk with any diagnostic (or therapeutic) intervention that uses radiation. Your referring physician has been trained to weigh up the risks and benefits of the dose of radiation you will receive in the examination that has been requested for you.

What do you need to do during the examination?

When you attend for your examination in the CT department, your CT practitioner will tell you exactly what you need to do. Depending on the examination you need, this will change how we carry out your examination. Most examinations will be carried out with you laying on the CT table with your head towards the CT scanner. The practitioner will ensure you are in the right position for your scan.

There is a chance that for your particular scan, you may need to have a canula placed in your hand, wrist or elbow to allow for the administration of an intravenous contrast agent - this can show certain anatomical structures in more detail. The placement of the canula can be painful, however the administration of the contrast should not be - if it is, you should tell the CT practitioner immediately. When the contrast agent goes into your arm, you may get a strange sensation going down your arm and through your body which can sometimes result in the feeling that you have wet yourself, please be assured, you haven't!

It is very important that when the practitioner is happy with your positioning, you stay extremely still until they tell you to move again. Moving before or during your imaging may result in an un-diagnostic CT scan which would mean it needs to be taken again, resulting in you receiving more radiation.

After your CT, the practitioner will confirm you know where and how to get your results. If you were changed into an CT gown for your examination, you will now be able to get changed back into your normal clothes and carry on with your day as normal.

What will the practitioner do during the examination?

Your CT practitioner will firstly confirm that you are the correct person for this examination, they will ask you to confirm your details and to confirm you understand what you are in the department for - these are standard questions that we must ask everybody. They (or a member of the team) may also place a canula in your hand, wrist or elbow (though this doesn't always happen) depending on the type of scan you are having.

They will then position you for your CT examination, they will tell you where you need to be for your imaging (e.g. laying or sitting down), and they may need to touch you to ensure you are in the correct place. Once they are happy with your position, you will be asked to stay very still while they go into the control room to take your scan. Once your scan has been taken, they will stay behind the screen for a few minutes to ensure they have everything they need on your picture (this includes ensuring all your personal details have been transferred over correctly, that you haven't moved during imaging, that all of the area of interest is on the image and that the reporters will be able to make a firm diagnosis from your scan) this can take some time, and you should try and stay in the same position while the practitioner is doing this just in case they need another image to aid in a better diagnosis.

If you have had a canula for your scan, the practitioner will remove this before you leave the department however they may leave it in for a little while after your scan just in case you have a reaction to the contrast agent.
